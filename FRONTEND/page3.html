Langar: A Tradition of Selfless Service in Punjab

Langar is a community kitchen service that is a prominent part of the Sikh tradition. It is organized to provide free meals to all, regardless of religion, caste, gender, or social status.

Why Organize Langar in Punjab?

The Langar system, a hallmark of Sikhism, was initiated by Guru Nanak Dev Ji, the founder of Sikhism, and later institutionalized by the subsequent Sikh Gurus. Here's a comprehensive explanation:

Guru Nanak Dev Ji (1469-1539) Initiation of Langar: Guru Nanak Dev Ji, the first Sikh Guru, laid the foundation of the Langar system. He emphasized the principles of equality, community service, and sharing. Guru Nanak's teachings rejected the caste system and social hierarchies prevalent in society at that time. Sacha Sauda Incident: A significant event that highlights Guru Nanak's commitment to these principles is the "Sacha Sauda" (True Bargain) incident. When his father gave him money to do business, Guru Nanak used the money to feed hungry saints and poor people, thus demonstrating the value of selfless service and charity.


Guru Angad Dev Ji (1504-1552) Promotion of Langar: Guru Angad Dev Ji, the second Sikh Guru, continued and promoted the Langar system. He ensured that it became an integral part of the Sikh community. Under his guidance, Langar became a regular feature at the Sikh congregations, reinforcing the principles of equality and humility.


Guru Amar Das Ji (1479-1574) Institutionalization of Langar: Guru Amar Das Ji, the third Sikh Guru, played a crucial role in institutionalizing the Langar system. He made it mandatory for anyone wishing to meet him to first partake in Langar, regardless of their social or economic status. This practice, known as "Pangat," ensured that all people sat together in rows, ate the same food, and experienced the sense of equality and community.


Guru Ram Das Ji (1534-1581) and Subsequent Gurus Expansion and Solidification: Guru Ram Das Ji, the fourth Guru, and the subsequent Gurus continued to expand and solidify the Langar system. They established new centers and ensured that the tradition of Langar was maintained and promoted. Key Principles of the Langar System Equality: Everyone, regardless of caste, creed, gender, or social status, sits together on the floor and eats the same food, symbolizing the equality of all people. Community Service (Seva): Langar is prepared and served by volunteers, emphasizing the importance of selfless service and humility. Sharing and Compassion: The concept of sharing resources and providing for those in need is central to Langar. It embodies the Sikh principles of compassion and generosity. Modern Day Langar Global Presence: Today, the Langar system operates in Gurdwaras (Sikh temples) worldwide, providing free meals to millions of people every day. Humanitarian Efforts: In addition to serving regular meals, Sikh communities organize Langar during natural disasters, protests, and other emergencies, extending their principles of service and compassion to a broader humanitarian context.


  • Promotes equality and humility.
  • Ensures no one goes hungry.
  • Fosters a sense of community and togetherness.
